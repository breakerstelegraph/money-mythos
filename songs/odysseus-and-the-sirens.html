Spoken Word

Odysseus
and the Sirens

On Temptation · On Restraint

There was a man. Once. A Greek who knew his only weakness before he met it.
He knew the song would come. And that he would not have the strength to refuse any bright shore already burning in his mind.
So he chose, in cold blood, what the heart would never choose — and he bound himself.
Said: let my ears stay open. Let my body strain, while my mind stays faithful.
No. That man is still dying. And the coast draws near.
The Song
Softly it rises now — over the glittering foam, sweet as a childhood dream. Sharp as a needle. My name in its breath, my fears in its fragrance.
It showed a finger, curling under my wrist. Why does desire dress itself in such tender colors? Why must the snare shimmer like salvation?
I feel it brushing the hollows of my heart — telling me I was born only for this surrender.
The Answer
All just to show: I'll never touch your hands. I'll never hold you. Shine within my longing like a lantern in the fog. You'll be free. I will be lost. If I were free, I would be gone.
So let me be the man who sees before the oars break, before my treacherous arms reach the wave. Let her ripple through my skin. Where music claws my mind — if I must tremble, scream — please let it be in joy. Not in your arms.
Not yet. While reason still can speak. Bind the storm inside my veins before it shatters.
I tie myself to the mast. Doubting the call. At the burning in my wrist — now burn the fire in my blood.
Let the oars mirror on. I will rage and I will rise. I will curse the straining hands that keep me to my course.
But when the tide has turned and silence lets us breathe — these little rope-burn marks will sing the truth song in me.
